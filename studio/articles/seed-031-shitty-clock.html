Shitty Clock, Always Late

A clock falls off the wall. Nearly hits the mother-in-law. The husband has opinions about the clock's punctuality, not the near-miss.

The Joke

"My wife said the clock fell off the wall and almost hit her mother."

"Shitty clock. Always late."

How It Was Made

The Setup: A warm suburban living room where a wall clock has just fallen, leaving a bare nail and dust ring on the wall with a cracked clock face on the floor below. Zoe rushes through the story of the near-miss while Tasha — the mother-in-law — sits on the couch in the background touching her temple, radiating the quiet indignation of a woman who expects better from the universe.

The Punchline: Jerry enters from the kitchen doorway with a coffee mug and complete deadpan stillness. No expression. No emotion. Just a flat assessment: the clock is shitty and always late. The humor lands not from anger but from his total refusal to treat the near-injury as noteworthy.

The Creative Process

This joke went through two significant creative revisions:

First greenlight (Concept B): A news anchor format where Jerry delivers the punchline as breaking news. Production review: it was radio disguised as video. The audience never sees the clock, the wife, or the mother-in-law. Reversed. — Jerry Gold, reluctantly admitting the cheaper option was the wrong one
Revised greenlight (Concept A): A domestic scene where every story element is physically visible. The clock is on the floor. The gap is on the wall. Zoe and Tasha are both on screen. Jerry reads the situation and chooses to deliver his verdict about the object, not the people. — Jerry Gold, doing his job correctly this time

Production: Two Vince attempts. The first had a mystery character in the opening scene and Jerry was smiling during the punchline — neither scripted. QA caught both. Second attempt: empty room for the hook, deadpan Jerry for the punchline. Passed at 57/70.

Cast Notes

Jerry Gold delivers the verdict with zero affect. His deadpan is not an act — it's a diagnostic assessment of clock performance.

Zoe Sparks carries the setup with genuine alarm. She is not performing concern. She is concerned.

Tasha Trend grounds the scene from the background. She brings her inherent self-importance to bear. The universe owes her better than a falling clock, and her expression says so.

Why This Joke Works

Misaligned emotional registers. The wife is terrified. The mother-in-law is indignant. The husband has opinions about punctuality devices. The word "shitty" gives the joke an edge that a cleaned-up version loses entirely — it sells the disappointment.
