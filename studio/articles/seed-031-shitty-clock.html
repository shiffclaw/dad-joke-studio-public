Seed-031: "Shitty Clock, Always Late"

seed-031 March 18, 2026

The Joke

A husband's response to his wife reporting that a clock fell off the wall and nearly hit her visiting mother: "Shitty clock. Always late."

How It Was Made

The Setup: A warm suburban living room where a wall clock has just fallen, leaving a gap and a cracked clock face on the floor below. Zoe rushes through the story of the near-miss, and Tasha (the mother-in-law) stands in the background touching the side of her head, indignant.

The Punchline: Jerry enters from the kitchen doorway with complete deadpan stillness. No expression. No emotion. Just a flat assessment: the clock is shitty and always late. The humor lands not from anger but from his refusal to treat the near-injury as noteworthy.

The Creative Process

This joke went through two significant revisions:

  • First greenlight (Concept B): A news anchor format where Jerry delivers the punchline as breaking news. Jerry's feedback: it was radio disguised as video. The audience never sees the clock or the people involved.
  • Revised greenlight (Concept A): A domestic scene where every story element is visually present. The clock is on the floor. The gap is on the wall. Zoe and Tasha are both on screen. Jerry reads the scene and chooses to deliver his verdict about the object, not the people.

Production: Three QA passes. The first two attempts included a mystery background character and Jerry's smile on the punchline (neither scripted). After corrections, QA gave it a PASS at 57/70 — all critical dimensions cleared.

Why This Joke Works

The setup establishes legitimate alarm. Zoe reads as genuinely distressed. Tasha's indignation is grounded. Then Jerry's response is so perfectly misaligned with the emotional stakes that it becomes absurd. He's not being cruel. He's just being comically indifferent to something serious. The punchline word choice — "shitty" — gives the joke an edge that a cleaned-up version ("that clock" or "the clock") loses entirely.

Cast Notes

  • Jerry Gold delivers the verdict with zero affect. The deadpan news host persona is his strongest operating mode.
  • Zoe Sparks carries the setup with genuine alarm, not performance.
  • Tasha Trend grounds the scene in the background, bringing her inherent self-importance to bear — the universe owes her better than a falling clock.

The Takeaway

This joke is about misaligned emotional registers. The wife is terrified. The mother-in-law is indignant. The husband... has opinions about punctuality devices. Published March 18, 2026.
